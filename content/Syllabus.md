## Description
This course examines the design and implementation of scalable systems, with emphasis on modern web-facing software and services. Topics may include client-server architecture, HTTP, APIs, data modeling, storage systems, state management, concurrency, performance, reliability, deployment, and security. The course is hands-on and project-based: students will design, implement, test, and evaluate substantial software artifacts while practicing technical communication, debugging, and iterative development.

## Prerequisites and Assumed Background
- **Formal prerequisite:** [Official prerequisite language]
- **Assumed background:** Students should be comfortable with programming, debugging, command-line tools, and reading technical documentation.
- **Recommended background:** Prior experience with JavaScript, TypeScript, Python, Git, SQL, and web development concepts is helpful, but the instructor should clarify what is required versus merely recommended.

## Learning Outcomes
By the end of the course, students should be able to:

- Explain the core architectural ideas behind scalable software systems.
- Build and debug nontrivial software that integrates multiple components, tools, or services.
- Evaluate design tradeoffs involving performance, reliability, correctness, maintainability, and security.
- Use version control, testing, and structured development workflows effectively.
- Communicate technical decisions clearly in code, documentation, and discussion.
- Work productively within the collaboration model defined for each assignment.

## Submission Rules
- Unless otherwise specified, all work must be submitted electronically through the designated course platform by the stated deadline.
- The authoritative deadline is the timestamp recorded by the submission system, in the course time zone.
- Students are responsible for uploading the correct files, in the correct format, before the deadline.
- Submissions must be readable, complete, and runnable in the required environment.
- If a repository is required, students must push their final version before the deadline and ensure the instructor or autograder has access.
- Students should keep backups of all work and commit changes regularly.

## Late Work Policy
- The syllabus should define one clear late policy and use it consistently.
- **Option A:** Each assignment may be submitted up to [X hours / days] late with a penalty of [penalty].
- **Option B:** Students receive [N] late tokens or grace days to use across the term.
- **Option C:** No late work is accepted except under approved circumstances.
- The chosen policy should state whether weekends count, whether deadlines are hard cutoffs, and whether projects or exams have different rules.
- Unless otherwise stated, last-minute technical issues on a student's own machine are not automatic grounds for an extension.

## Collaboration Policy
- Unless an assignment explicitly permits group work, students must complete the work individually.
- Students may discuss high-level concepts with classmates only to the extent allowed by the assignment.
- Students may not share code, written solutions, solution outlines, test cases, screenshots of answers, or step-by-step solution strategies unless explicitly permitted.
- Students must acknowledge collaborators, external references, and any other allowed sources of help.
- Copying or closely paraphrasing another person's work, even with minor edits, is not permitted.

## Generative AI Policy
- The syllabus should state explicitly whether generative AI tools are prohibited, limited, or allowed for each assignment type.
- If AI tools are allowed in any form, students must disclose what tools they used, how they used them, and what parts of the final submission were influenced by those tools.
- If AI tools are not allowed for a given assignment, using them to generate code, text, tests, explanations, or solution strategies for submission is prohibited.
- Students remain responsible for understanding, validating, and defending every part of any submitted work.
- Undisclosed or prohibited AI use may be treated as an academic integrity violation.
